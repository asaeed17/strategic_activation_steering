Here's the reading list, organized by what will actually save you from pitfalls vs. what's nice
  background. I'm flagging the specific issues each paper addresses for your project.

  ---
  Must-Read (directly relevant, will prevent mistakes)

  1. Panickssery et al. 2024 — "Steering Llama 2 via Contrastive Activation Addition"

  - Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.06681 | Code: GitHub (linked in paper appendix)
  - Why: This is the method your project implements. Your codebase's mean-diff approach is CAA.
  - Pitfall it prevents: They found that large steering magnitudes degrade open-ended text quality
  as rated by both GPT-4 and human evaluators. Your initial run used alpha=20 which is absurdly high
   — the search found alpha≈6 works better. They also show that steering vectors from base models
  transfer to RLHF'd models, and that negative coefficients reverse behavior (relevant for P6's
  negative-alpha experiment).

  2. Zou et al. 2023 — "Representation Engineering: A Top-Down Approach to AI Transparency"

  - Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01405 | Code:
  https://github.com/andyzoujm/representation-engineering
  - Why: This is the PCA method in your extract_vectors.py. Their code repo has reference
  implementations you can validate against.
  - Pitfall it prevents: They discuss sign ambiguity in PCA — your code already handles this (line
  307 in extract_vectors.py), but it's worth understanding why.

  3. Hao et al. 2025 — "Patterns and Mechanisms of Contrastive Activation Engineering" (ICLR 2025
  Workshop)

  - Paper: https://openreview.net/pdf?id=FZk9oWvZm2
  - Why: This is the most directly useful for your evaluation work. Key findings:
    - ~80 contrastive pairs is the point of diminishing returns — you have 12 per dimension, which
  is low. P1/P5's task to expand to 20+ matters.
    - Steering vectors harm overall model perplexity — you should measure this.
    - Larger models are more resistant to steering-induced degradation — relevant for interpreting
  results on Qwen 3B vs 7B.
    - CAE is only reliably effective on in-distribution contexts — your contrastive pairs are
  generic negotiation, but your evaluation is Craigslist-specific. This gap may explain weak
  results.

  4. "A Sober Look at Steering Vectors for LLMs" (Alignment Forum post)

  - Link:
  https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/QQP4nq7TXg89CJGBh/a-sober-look-at-steering-vectors-for-llms
  - Why: Honest assessment of failure modes. Key warnings:
    - Steering often degrades overall performance, fluency, and coherence — equivalent to halving
  pre-training compute in some cases (Cooper Stickland et al.)
    - Your LLM judge should explicitly rate coherence/fluency alongside negotiation quality to catch
   this.

  5. "Activation Steering in 2026: A Practitioner's Field Guide"

  - Link: https://subhadipmitra.com/blog/2026/activation-steering-field-guide/
  - Why: Extremely practical. Key insights relevant to your project:
    - Steering effects fade after ~300-500 tokens — your negotiations run 8-10 turns, so late turns
  may be unsteered. Your per-turn metrics should check whether the effect diminishes over the
  conversation.
    - Multi-vector stacking is fragile — if P3 tries combining firmness + anchoring vectors at the
  same layer, it will likely interfere. Inject at different layers instead.
    - Different models have different alpha tolerances — Qwen is the most tolerant, which matches
  your results.

  ---
  Negotiation-Specific (for evaluation design)

  6. Xia et al. 2024 — "Measuring Bargaining Abilities of LLMs"

  - Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.15813
  - Why: They formalize bargaining as an asymmetric incomplete-information game (which is exactly
  what your CraigslistBargains setup is). Key finding: playing Buyer is harder than Seller, and
  increasing model size doesn't improve Buyer performance. Your data shows the same —
  buyer_score=0.63 vs seller_score=0.37 in the baseline. This isn't a bug, it's a known asymmetry.
  - For P4: Their evaluation metrics are more sophisticated than yours. Look at their OG-Narrator
  method — it separates strategic decisions from natural language generation, which is conceptually
  what steering does too.

  7. Wang et al. 2025 — "Evaluating Multi-Turn Bargain Skills in LLM-Based Seller Agents"

  - Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.06341
  - Why: They propose turn-level evaluation grounded in Theory of Mind rather than outcome-only
  metrics. This is exactly the gap in your current evaluation — you only measure final price. Their
  framework evaluates whether the agent correctly tracks buyer intent across turns. Highly relevant
  for your LLM judge design.

  8. "LLM Agents for Bargaining with Utility-based Feedback" (2025)

  - Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.22998
  - Why: They propose HAMBA (Human-Aligned Metric for Bargaining) which decomposes performance into
  Consumer Surplus, Negotiation Power, and Acquisition Ratio. This is more principled than your
  current score_deal() which only measures price split. Consider adapting their metric framework.

  9. He et al. 2018 — "Decoupling Strategy and Generation in Negotiation Dialogues"

  - Paper: https://aclanthology.org/D18-1256/
  - Why: This is the CraigslistBargains dataset paper. Read it to understand the data quirks — known
   issues with crossed targets, items with weird pricing, category imbalances. P5's data audit task
  exists for a reason.

  Background (understand once, cite in paper)

  10. Turner et al. 2023 — "Steering Language Models With Activation Engineering"

  - Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.10248
  - Why: The original activation addition paper (single-pair, GPT-2). CAA improved on this by
  averaging over many pairs.

  11. Li et al. 2023 — "Inference-Time Intervention: Eliciting Truthful Answers from a Language
  Model"

  - Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.03341
  - Why: They independently derived the same technique for truthfulness. Relevant for P2's probing
  work — they use linear probes to identify which attention heads encode truthfulness, then steer
  those heads specifically. Your P1 task on attention-head extraction comes from this.

  12. Jorgensen et al. 2023 — "Improving Activation Steering with Mean-Centring"

  - Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.03813
  - Why: Mean-centering is essentially what P1's "whitened mean diff" task is about. This paper
  shows it helps, which motivates that task.

  ---
  Summary of pitfalls to watch for in your P4 work specifically

  1. Measure coherence degradation, not just negotiation success. A steered agent that "wins" by
  producing weird repetitive text isn't a real result.
  2. Track metrics per-turn, not just per-game. Steering effects may fade over the conversation
  (300-500 token decay).
  3. The buyer/seller asymmetry is real and known. Don't report aggregate scores without breaking
  down by role.
  4. 12 pairs per dimension is probably too few. Literature suggests diminishing returns around 80.
  Flag this as a limitation.
  5. In-distribution vs out-of-distribution matters. Your pairs are generic negotiation but your
  evaluation is Craigslist-specific. The LLM judge should check whether steering produces
  contextually appropriate responses, not just firm/yielding ones.
