P1 — EXTRACTION METHODS
Paper section: Extraction Methods
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Add whitened mean diff to extract_vectors.py
  The standard mean diff picks up noise from general model variance (sentence
  length, token frequency). Whitening isolates the actual firmness signal, giving
  us a cleaner direction to steer with and likely a stronger negotiator.

- Add contrastive decoding direction to extract_vectors.py
  Instead of contrasting hidden states, contrast what the model predicts next.
  This may capture a more action-oriented representation of firmness, closer to
  what actually drives negotiation behaviour.

- Add random direction control to extract_vectors.py
  If steering with a random direction produces similar negotiation advantage, our
  whole approach is questionable. This is the sanity check that validates
  everything else.

- Extract directions from attention heads rather than residual stream hiddens
  Firmness may be concentrated in specific attention heads rather than spread
  across the full hidden state. Extracting per-head directions and comparing them
  to full-layer directions tells us where in the transformer the concept actually
  lives.

- Extract directions using activation differences during generation
  Instead of using curated pairs, run the model freely on negotiation prompts and
  collect activations when it spontaneously produces firm vs yielding language.
  The direction extracted from naturalistic behaviour may generalise better than
  one extracted from hand-crafted pairs.

- Extract directions via SVD on a large set of negotiation transcripts
  No pairs needed. Collect 200+ negotiation utterances, extract all their hidden
  states, run SVD, and see whether the top singular vectors align with
  interpretable negotiation dimensions like firmness or concession. Completely
  unsupervised — if it works it means we don't need the pairs pipeline at all.

- Expand negotiation_steering_pairs.json with 20+ new pairs per dimension
  Even with the new methods above, the pair-based methods still benefit from more
  diverse pairs. More diverse pairs means the vector generalises better across
  different negotiation contexts.

P3 — APPLICATION STRATEGIES
Paper section: Application Strategies
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Add DynamicAlphaScheduler to apply_steering.py
  A real negotiator doesn't stay equally firm throughout. Ramping alpha up when
  the opponent pushes hard and down after making an offer mimics genuine
  negotiation strategy and should produce better outcomes than constant steering.

- Add TokenSelectiveHook to apply_steering.py
  Applying the direction on every token including filler words is wasteful and
  disrupts fluency. Concentrating the steering on the tokens where the model
  commits to a price should make the intervention more precise and less likely
  to cause incoherence.

- Add ProjectionHook to apply_steering.py
  At high alpha the additive hook inflates activation norms and breaks the model.
  Projecting the direction orthogonally preserves norm, meaning we can push
  firmness harder without the model collapsing into gibberish, giving us a
  stronger negotiator at the same coherence level.

Paper section: Evaluation Framework + Results & Analysis
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Add offer trajectory, concession rate, hedge word count, turns to deal, first
  offer distance to run_game() and score_deal()
  Price outcome alone is a lagging indicator. These metrics tell us how the
  steered agent is winning: is it anchoring better, conceding less, closing
  faster? Without this we can't understand what the steering is actually doing
  to negotiation behaviour.

- Write llm_judge.py — takes a results JSON, hits an LLM API, rates each
  transcript on firmness, persuasiveness, and naturalness
  Price outcome can be gamed by extreme anchoring that happens to work. The judge
  catches whether the agent is actually negotiating well or just getting lucky on
  easy scenarios.

P6 — CONCEPT PATCHING & INTERPRETABILITY
Paper section: Discussion & Limitations
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Write concept_patching.py — mid-game overwrite of hidden state components along
  the steering direction, measure change in hedge word count, numeric mentions,
  and offer movement
  This is the most direct evidence that our vectors causally control negotiation
  behaviour rather than just correlating with it. It's what lets us say "when we
  inject firmness into the model, it negotiates more firmly" rather than "we
  steered something and the price went up."

- Run probe-steering consistency experiment — collect hidden states from steered
  agent at various alpha values, measure whether behavioural shift tracks the
  intended direction
  Confirms that increasing alpha actually increases firmness in the representation,
  not just adds noise. Establishes that we have a dial we can actually turn.

- Run negative alpha experiment — 30 games with negative alpha, document whether
  agent becomes measurably more yielding
  If negative alpha makes the agent worse and positive alpha makes it better, the
  direction is genuinely causal. This is the cleanest possible evidence that the
  steering vector is doing what we claim.

- Run probe degradation test — sweep alpha 1 to 50, measure how hidden state
  geometry changes
  Finds the ceiling of how hard we can steer before the representation breaks
  down. Tells P3 exactly where the useful range of alpha is for the application
  strategies.
