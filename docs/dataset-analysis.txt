  Negotiation Datasets — Ranked by Fit

  1. Deal or No Deal (Lewis et al., 2017)

  - Paper: Deal or No Deal? End-to-End Learning for Negotiation Dialogues (EMNLP 2017, Facebook AI Research)
  - Size: ~5,808 dialogues
  - What's negotiated: Division of items (books, hats, balls) with different hidden utility values per
  player
  - Key metrics: Individual utility, Pareto efficiency, agreement rate, number of turns
  - Download: https://github.com/facebookresearch/end-to-end-negotiator
  - Why it's the best fit for you: Clear quantitative scoring. Each party has a known value function, so you
   can directly measure whether steering toward "firmness" actually improves utility or whether
  "cooperation" increases Pareto efficiency. The multi-item structure lets you test whether steering affects
   strategic reasoning (not just price anchoring). This is the dataset where your hypothesis is most cleanly
   testable.
  - Limitation: The dialogues are relatively simple and short. Written in 2017, so the language patterns are
   human-from-that-era, not LLM-style.

  2. CraigslistBargain (He et al., 2018)

  - Paper: Decoupling Strategy and Generation in Negotiation Dialogues (EMNLP 2018, Stanford)
  - Size: ~6,682 dialogues
  - What's negotiated: Price of Craigslist items (buyer wants low, seller wants high)
  - Key metrics: Sale-to-list ratio, agreement rate, buyer/seller advantage
  - Download: https://github.com/stanfordnlp/cocoa (under the craigslistbargain directory)
  - Why it's good: More naturalistic than Deal or No Deal. Single-issue (price) makes analysis cleaner — you
   can directly measure whether "empathy" steering gives better or worse prices. Also has strategy
  annotations in some versions.
  - Limitation: Single-issue negotiation is less rich for testing multiple behavioral dimensions. The metric
   space is essentially one-dimensional (final price relative to listing).

  3. CASINO (Chawla et al., 2021)

  - Paper: CASINO: A Corpus of Campsite Negotiation Dialogues (NAACL 2021)
  - Size: ~1,030 dialogues
  - What's negotiated: Campsite items (food, water, firewood) with priority rankings
  - Key metrics: Points scored, Pareto efficiency, strategy annotations
  - Download: Available on GitHub — https://github.com/kushalchawla/CaSiNo
  - Why it's interesting: Has explicit strategy annotations (e.g., "elicit preferences", "show empathy",
  "make trade-off") which map directly to your behavioral steering dimensions. You could validate whether
  your "empathy vector" actually produces dialogue that gets labeled as empathetic by CASINO's annotation
  scheme.
  - Limitation: Small dataset. Only 1,030 dialogues. Fine for evaluation scenarios, not great for
  large-scale analysis.

  4. NegotiationToM (Bianchi et al., 2024) / Recent LLM-era benchmarks

  - Several recent papers (2023–2025) have started evaluating LLMs directly on negotiation. Search for
  "NegoBench" and "LLM negotiation benchmark." These are more relevant to your framing since they already
  have LLM-vs-LLM or LLM-vs-human evaluation protocols.
  - Worth checking: NegoBench (if published) and any papers from ICLR/ACL 2024 on LLM negotiation
  evaluation.

  5. PersuasionForGood (Wang et al., 2019)

  - Persuasion for charity donation — not strictly negotiation but adjacent. Useful only if you expand your
  behavioral dimensions to include persuasion-style steering. I'd skip it to stay focused.
